## What? (Description)

In Unit 3, I studied the fundamental principles of computer science, with a particular focus on algorithms, algorithmic thinking, and computational efficiency. The unit introduced key concepts such as algorithm design, data handling, and time complexity using Big‑O notation. It also explored theoretical foundations of computer science, including propositional logic, set theory, the Turing Machine, and Lambda Calculus, which together define what problems can be computed and how solutions are formally represented.

As part of the unit, I completed and submitted the Algorithm Analysis Activity and the Algorithm Analysis and Implementation assignment. This involved implementing and comparing two sorting algorithms, Bubble Sort and QuickSort, using Python. I benchmarked their performance for different input sizes, generated a performance graph, and produced a written analysis discussing algorithm behaviour and scalability. I also attended the Unit 3 seminar on principles of computer science and algorithmic thinking, which reinforced concepts such as computational thinking, problem decomposition, and efficiency analysis. In addition, I continued participating in the Collaborative Learning Discussion during Week 3.

---

## So what? (Interpretation)

This unit helped me understand why algorithm choice is critical when working with large datasets. While simple algorithms such as Bubble Sort are easy to understand, their O(n²) time complexity makes them unsuitable for large‑scale systems. In contrast, algorithms like QuickSort demonstrate how divide‑and‑conquer approaches significantly improve performance, with average‑case O(n log n) behaviour that scales more effectively as data grows.

From a professional perspective, these concepts directly relate to my work in telecom automation and enterprise systems. Activities such as billing processing, data reconciliation, provisioning, and monitoring often involve large volumes of records where inefficient algorithms can increase processing time and resource usage. Understanding Big‑O notation and algorithmic efficiency allows me to make more informed technical decisions and to better evaluate the performance implications of different implementation choices.

I plan to use Big‑O reasoning as a practical tool when selecting or reviewing algorithms, especially in systems that process large or growing datasets.

The foundations covered in this unit will be particularly valuable in future modules related to artificial intelligence, optimisation, and enterprise‑scale computing, where performance and computational efficiency are essential.
