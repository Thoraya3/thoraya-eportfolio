# Launch into Computing – Unit 6 Reflection
## Principles of Artificial Intelligence (AI) I

---

## What? (Description)

In Unit 6, I studied the foundational principles of Artificial Intelligence (AI) and how AI techniques are applied within modern enterprise systems. The unit focused on distinguishing between Artificial Intelligence, Machine Learning (ML), and Deep Learning, and explored how these approaches are used for automation, prediction, and decision support. The Lecturecast and reading materials also emphasised the growing impact of AI across industries and the importance of addressing ethical, legal, and social implications.

As part of this unit, I completed the required reading on ethical AI development, including topics such as bias, transparency, and accountability. I also completed the AI Model Activity and the assignment on implementing and evaluating a simple AI model. Using Python and the Scikit‑learn library, I implemented a supervised machine learning classification model, trained it on a publicly available dataset, and evaluated its performance using metrics such as accuracy, precision, and recall. The activity included analysing the model’s behaviour, identifying limitations, and considering ethical risks associated with real‑world deployment.

---

## So what? (Interpretation)

This unit helped me understand that AI systems are not just technical artefacts, but socio‑technical systems that can significantly influence decision‑making in organisations. Learning to evaluate model performance using multiple metrics highlighted that accuracy alone is not sufficient, especially in domains where different types of errors have unequal consequences. Precision and recall provide additional insight into how reliable and fair a model’s predictions are.

From a professional perspective, these concepts are directly relevant to my work in enterprise and telecom environments, where AI is increasingly used for automation, analytics, and operational optimisation. The discussion of ethical challenges reinforced the risks of biased data, lack of explainability, and unclear accountability when AI systems are integrated into business processes. Understanding these risks is essential to ensuring that AI solutions are trustworthy, compliant with regulations, and aligned with organisational responsibility.

---

## What next? (Action)

Going forward, I will approach AI adoption with a stronger emphasis on evaluation, transparency, and governance. This includes selecting appropriate models, validating them with suitable metrics, and considering ethical implications from the design stage rather than as an afterthought. I also plan to continue developing my practical skills in machine learning while remaining critical of the limitations and assumptions behind AI models.

Academically, I will continue documenting AI activities and reflections in my e‑portfolio to link theoretical understanding with practical implementation. The knowledge gained in this unit provides a solid foundation for more advanced AI modules, particularly those involving applied machine learning, responsible AI, and enterprise‑scale intelligent systems.
