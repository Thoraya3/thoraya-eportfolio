# Launch into Computing – Unit 7 Reflection
## Principles of Artificial Intelligence (AI) II

---

## What? (Description)

In Unit 7, I continued exploring the principles of Artificial Intelligence (AI) with a stronger focus on enterprise application, risk, and governance. The unit built on the foundations from Unit 6 by examining how AI, Machine Learning, and data-driven systems are deployed at scale and the security, ethical, and organisational challenges that arise from their use. The Lecturecast and seminar materials covered data science workflows, data storage architectures (SQL, NoSQL, and cloud databases), Big Data processing frameworks, and the importance of statistics and probability in evaluating AI-driven decisions.

As part of this unit, I completed the required reading on ethical AI use and participated in the Unit 7 seminar on Data Science, Storage Solutions, and Big Data Processing. I also completed the Security Risk Assessment Report activity, where I identified potential threats and vulnerabilities in AI-enabled enterprise systems, including phishing, malware, and data breaches. The task required analysing risks at a system level and proposing practical mitigation measures such as encryption, multi-factor authentication, and continuous monitoring.

---

## So what? (Interpretation)

This unit reinforced the idea that AI systems must be evaluated not only for technical performance but also for security, reliability, and ethical impact. Understanding data storage choices (ACID vs BASE, SQL vs NoSQL, cloud vs on-premises) highlighted how architectural decisions directly affect risk, compliance, and trust in AI-driven outcomes. The seminar discussions on statistics and probability further emphasised that AI predictions are inherently uncertain and that poor interpretation of data distributions or averages can lead to incorrect or biased decisions.

From a professional perspective, these insights are highly relevant to enterprise and telecom environments, where AI systems often process sensitive customer data and support automated decision-making. Security weaknesses or governance failures in such systems can have serious operational and regulatory consequences. The risk assessment activity helped me develop a more structured way of thinking about threats, vulnerabilities, and mitigations when AI is integrated into complex business systems.

---

## What next? (Action)

Going forward, I will apply a more holistic approach when evaluating AI solutions, considering not only accuracy and efficiency but also security controls, ethical implications, and organisational accountability. I plan to incorporate risk assessment and governance considerations early in the design and deployment of AI-enabled systems, particularly in regulated enterprise contexts.

Academically, I will continue to use reflective practice in my e-portfolio to connect AI theory with real-world system risks and decision-making. The learning from this unit provides a strong foundation for future modules focused on advanced AI, cybersecurity, and responsible deployment of intelligent systems at scale.
